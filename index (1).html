The Daguerreotype

In 1839, Louis Daguerre introduced the daguerreotype, the first widely recognized photographic process. This involved exposing a silver-coated copper plate to light, producing a highly detailed image. The daguerreotype was a breakthrough that made photography a commercially viable art form.

The Rise of Film

In the late 19th century, George Eastman revolutionized photography by introducing flexible roll film. This breakthrough allowed for more portable cameras and reduced the need for glass plates. Kodak's "Brownie" camera made photography accessible to the public, and film photography became the dominant medium for over a century.

Digital Photography

The introduction of digital sensors in the late 20th century marked a new era in photography. Digital cameras made it easier to capture, edit, and store photos, eliminating the need for film processing. The rapid evolution of digital photography technology has shaped modern photography, from consumer smartphones to professional DSLR cameras.
